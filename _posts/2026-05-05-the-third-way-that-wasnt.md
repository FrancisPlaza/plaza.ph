In Bac Ninh and Thai Nguyen, an hour north of Hanoi, Samsung makes roughly half its smartphones. In 1986, when Vietnam's Communist Party launched Doi Moi, Vietnamese were poorer than Filipinos. Today they are richer.

<!--more-->

The conventional reading in Manila is that Vietnam abandoned communism. The conventional reading in *Philawsophia* is sharper. Bernardo declares the communist experiment 'fizzled'. Russia turned right, China embraced markets and the Soviet Union collapsed. *Calalang v. Williams* supplied the Philippine alternative. Social justice, wrote Justice Laurel, was 'neither communism, nor despotism, nor atomism, nor anarchy'. The third way was meant to be ours.

The data dissents. Vietnam's GDP per capita overtook the Philippines in 2020. Its poverty rate sits below 6%; ours hovers above 15%. China lifted 800m people out of poverty between 1980 and 2020. That's more than the entire population of Latin America. The Philippines, which never had to abandon communism because it never embraced it, ended the same period with a political class still dominated by a handful of dynastic families and one that has refused, for nearly forty years, to pass the enabling law that would give effect to the Constitution's own ban on political dynasties.

Marx did not win. He was wrong about communism. But he was right about a more uncomfortable point. Material conditions shape consciousness, not the reverse. Article XIII does not build a productive base. The Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Program does not. Factories do.

*De Ramas v. CAR* confirms the diagnosis. Justice Labrador wrote that the Agricultural Tenancy Act was passed because 'the tenants forming the PKM… and, during the war, the Hukbalahap, rose in arms… as their only salvation from permanent thralldom'. The reform forestalled communism. But forestalling is not the same as fixing. The Huks were defeated. Tenancy fell only marginally. Vietnam, starting from a peasant economy, now attracts three times the foreign direct investment we do.

Manila does not need to envy Hanoi or Beijing. Both fail Justice Fernando's test in *People v. Lava*, that a democracy must defend itself democratically. We pass that test, but they have done more without it than we have with it.

The third way was a good idea. We simply never built it.
