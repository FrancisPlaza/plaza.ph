title:  "AI will change Philippine law faster than you think"

Somewhere in the US, [a transactional lawyer runs a two-person firm](https://x.com/zackbshapiro/status/2027389987444957625) that routinely outmanoeuvres teams ten times its size. His secret is mundane. He uploads a 40-page redlined agreement to an AI model, gets back a severity-rated analysis of every proposed change in minutes, applies tracked changes to the Word document without ever opening Word, and sends a polished response package. All the research, redline and cover e-mail done within the hour. Work that once kept a bullpen of associates up past midnight now fits between lunch and a client call.

A separate argument, from a corporate lawyer with decades of deal experience, is blunter still. [Large law firms, he writes, will not survive AI](https://x.com/deredleritt3r/status/2031558138562859510). Clients hire them for three things: specialist knowledge, the ability to throw bodies at high-volume document work and the credibility that comes with a marquee name on a high-stakes matter. AI is eroding all three. It delivers specialist knowledge faster than any associate can research it, processes thousands of documents without sleeping and, crucially, makes it possible for senior partners to do the work that matters without the army of juniors who used to justify their fees. The best partners, he predicts, will leave to start lean boutiques. The leverage model collapses. BigLaw hollows out.

These are American stories, told by American lawyers about an American market. But the forces they describe are structural, not geographic. And the Philippine legal profession, with its particular blend of concentration and institutional inertia, may be more exposed to these forces than anyone in it seems willing to admit.

Consider the starting conditions. The Philippines has roughly 60,000 practising lawyers for a population of 115 million. That is one lawyer for every 1,900 people, a ratio that flatters the supply side because the vast majority are concentrated in Metro Manila. Step outside the capital and access to competent legal counsel thins quickly. Filipinos in the provinces navigate land disputes, labour complaints and family law matters without a lawyer not because they choose to, but because they cannot find or afford one.

At the top of the market sit the big firms (the likes of SyCip, Romulo, ACCRA, VA, to name a few) serving the country's largest corporations, handling cross-border M&A, advising on regulatory matters before the BSP and the SEC. Below them stretches a broad middle of small partnerships and solo practitioners. They do everyday legal work at margins that leave little room to invest in technology or hire support staff. And the courts remain, for the most part, paper-bound. The Supreme Court's eCourt system has made progress, but anyone who has filed a motion in a regional trial court knows the reality well enough. Lawyers still print physical copies, use rubber stamps and end up with cases that drag on for years not because the law is complex but because the plumbing cannot move any faster.

This is the profession into which AI arrives not a sleek, digitised industry ready for its next upgrade, but one where the gap between what technology can do and what practitioners actually use is vast. And that gap is precisely what makes the disruption so potent.

The Philippine BigLaw model, like its American counterpart, runs on leverage. These firms have a handful of partners directing a much larger number of associates who do the research, draft the memos, review the documents, assemble the due diligence reports. AI compresses that pyramid. A partner who can instruct an AI model with the same precision she would use to brief a junior associate gets work product in minutes that would have taken days. The associate does not vanish from the picture, but the role changes: less drafting from scratch, more reviewing and refining AI output. Fewer associates per deal. Less billable time per transaction. Clients, particularly the sophisticated ones, will eventually do the arithmetic and ask why they are paying for a dozen juniors on a due diligence exercise when an AI-augmented team of three produces the same result faster.

But the more interesting part of the story is not what happens on Paseo de Roxas. It is what happens everywhere else. A solo practitioner in Marinduque handling SME contracts and regulatory filings currently competes at a structural disadvantage against Manila firms with deeper benches. AI narrows that gap dramatically. The same tools that let a two-person outfit in San Francisco go toe-to-toe with a white-shoe firm let a two-person outfit in Cebu compete with the biggest names in Makati. Document review, contract drafting, regulatory research, compliance mapping don't require a 200-lawyer office. They require a good lawyer with good tools and the skill to use them well. A provincial attorney advising a local cooperative on BSP circulars can now map the relevant regulations, cross-reference compliance requirements and draft the documentation without engaging a Manila specialist. A small firm running due diligence on a mid-market acquisition can process the data room in a fraction of the time and cost. A litigator preparing for trial can synthesise every relevant court decision on a doctrine, spot the inconsistencies, identify the strongest authorities. These are work that once demanded either a large team or an impossibly long weekend.

In a country where legal services cluster in one metropolis and demand is scattered across more than 7,500 islands, this is not a marginal improvement. It is a structural shift in who can deliver competent legal work and where. None of which removes the lawyer. AI does not exercise judgment, build client trust or stand up in court. But it collapses the distance between what a solo practitioner can deliver and what a large firm can and, in doing so, it rewrites the competitive map.

Now, if the practice of law is being reshaped this quickly, you might expect the institutions that produce lawyers to be preparing students for it.

In the Philippines, most law schools are not. Many are moving in the opposite direction. Legal education here remains anchored to a model built for a pre-digital profession. The Socratic method still dominates, and rightly so. Learning to think on your feet under pressure is genuinely valuable. But the resistance to technology runs far deeper than pedagogical tradition. Some professors require handwritten case digests, on the theory that writing by hand forces deeper engagement with the material. Others, during online classes, ask students to close their eyes when called upon to recite, replicating the pressure of a physical classroom through a method that is more theatrical than pedagogical.

AI use in academic work is broadly prohibited or, at least, discouraged; students caught using it can face disciplinary action. The implicit message is that technology is a shortcut, and shortcuts are dishonest. That position made sense when the worst a student could do was copy from a digest compilation. It makes far less sense when the technology is not a shortcut but a professional tool that practising lawyers abroad are already building their careers around. A law school that punishes students for learning to work with AI is not teaching discipline. It is teaching them to ignore the single most consequential development in legal practice in a generation.

The Legal Education Board, which sets curriculum standards, does not currently require instruction in legal technology, AI-assisted research or the ethical frameworks governing AI use in practice. There is no required course on technology competence, even as bar associations in other jurisdictions have begun treating it as an ethical obligation. The result is a pipeline that produces lawyers trained to think as though it were 1995. Sure, we produce lawyers that are strong on doctrine but are unequipped for the tools that will define how law is practised in 2030.

This is not an argument against rigour. Doctrinal mastery, legal reasoning, the ability to build and dismantle arguments under pressure. These remain the foundation. But they are necessary, not sufficient. A lawyer who can analyse a contract but cannot instruct an AI to do so at scale will be outpaced by one who can do both. The schools that recognise this early and weave AI literacy into their programmes as a core competency, not a curiosity, will produce graduates with an advantage that compounds year on year.

The Philippine legal profession will not transform overnight. Lawyers are cautious by training and by temperament. Courts move at their own pace. Regulation follows practice rather than leading it. But the economics are patient and relentless. AI makes legal work faster, cheaper and, when used well, more thorough. Clients will notice. The lawyers and firms that learn to instruct these tools with precision, that build workflows around them, that treat AI as an amplifier of judgment rather than a threat to their livelihood, will pull ahead steadily and then sharply. The ones that wait will find themselves competing against practitioners who deliver better work, more quickly, for less.

For a country of 115 million people and not nearly enough lawyers to serve them, though, the real promise is not efficiency for its own sake. It is the possibility that competent legal counsel might finally reach the parts of the archipelago it has never reached before. For me, that is worth building toward.
